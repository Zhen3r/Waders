Have you met... the Great Knots?

One of the greatest waders

Hi, come to know `Great Knot` (Calidris tenuirostris)! It is a small wader, but also a great long-distance migratory shorebird of the East Asian-Australasian Flyway.

Here is a great knot~ Her tag may say "XNC", but she is Beak Beak to the reseacher. As a "research assistant" of Deakin University, Australia, Beak Beak will carry a small solar-satellite device throughout this summer. Let's see how is she doing.


Beak-Beak's Home

(Actually, it's one of her homes.)

This is Beak Beak's home in Broome, Australia. A beautiful beach with lots of molluscs and insects.

Getting into the late March, it will soon be cold here... Maybe it's time for Beak-Beak to start!!

Journey with Wind

Leveraging the power of nature

Sometimes, Beak Beak can easily fly over the Indian Ocean and even the South China Sea with the help of the power of subtropical monsoon. With some good luck, the wind speed can reach up to 50 kilometers per hour.

It'll take Beak-Beak around 10-15 days to get to the east China, and get some rest there.

A BIG Meal!!

Swamp, Beach, and Marsh

Unknowingly, it's mid April. It's time to get some energy to move on. Yellow Sea is its chance to have a good meal on the journey. The beaches, swamps and marshes with lots of molluscs and insects are like a cafeteria for her.

Source: [EAAFP] World Migratory Bird Day 2020

With its beak full of receptors, it goes deep into the soil of 3-5cm and looks for its lunch. During the following month, she will constantly travel to and from the Yellow Sea of China and change different restaurants every day.

A restaurant on the brink of bankruptcy

Wetlands and Reclamation

It is worth noting that due to the excessive development in coastal regions around Yellow Sea, such good restaurants (wetlands) are gradually harder to find.

Studies show that over the past 30 years, the coastline of the Bohai Sea was moving towards offshore. The total area loss of the Yellow Sea adds upto 4063 km2. Thus, saddly, the population of Great Knot had declined by 58% in the past 30 years.

However, in 2018 the Chinese government announced a ban on further reclamation of coastal wetlands. This policy decision, taking many by surprise, effectively removed what was considered the biggest threat to migratory shorebirds in the Flyway.

Although it is still a long way to go to secure the future of the millions of migratory shorebirds, there is hope.

Home, Sweet Home

What a journey!

After 3 months of journey, Beak Beak finally arrives in in northeast Siberia, her home in the Northern Hemisphere. It is a large delta formed by Indigirka River, consisting of a number of streams and wetlands.

Source: Google Earth

Many waders form monogamous pairs, so does Beak Beak. She meets her husband . They find a tundra that is perfect for babies.

New Borns

Continuation of life

They exchange on the nest in the morning and in the evening so that their incubation rhythm follows a 24-hour day. Their children hatch after about three weeks of incubation and are able to walk and forage within a few hours of hatching. Beak Beak and her husband guide and brood their children together.
